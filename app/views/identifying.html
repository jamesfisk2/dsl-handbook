Identifying radicalisation in your education setting

1. Background to this guidance

Safeguarding individuals vulnerable to radicalisation depends on effective partnership. To demonstrate effective compliance with the duty, specified authorities (schools, colleges and further education independent training providers) must demonstrate evidence of productive co-operation, in particular with local Prevent co-ordinators, the police and local authorities, and co-ordination through existing multi-agency forums, for example Community Safety Partnerships.

Within each local authority, there are different referral routes for safeguarding children and young people from radicalisation and extremism. Effective partnerships will ensure that you understand these processes.

'School' means all schools whether maintained, non-maintained or independent schools (including academies, free schools and alternative provision academies), maintained nursery schools and individual referral units.

'Colleges and further education independent training providers' means further education colleges and sixth-form colleges as established under the Further and Higher Education Act 1992 and institutions designated as being within the further education sector, such as independent training providers, employer providers and adult education providers.

First and foremost, providers should consider the needs of children and young people at each stage of the safeguarding process. It is important to remember that providers should consider radicalisation concerns in line with their existing safeguarding processes, skills and experience.

Settings are required to have due regard to prevent people from being drawn into terrorism.

The Prevent duty guidance for schools and childcare providers requires providers to:

  • show a general understanding of the risks affecting children and young people in this area and a specific understanding of how to identify individual children who may be at risk of radicalisation and what to do to support them
  • understand local risks and threats

1.1 Vulnerability factors

There is no single way of identifying whether a child or young person is likely to be susceptible to an extremist ideology or vulnerable to radicalisation but there are several vulnerability factors that could contribute to this. It is best practice for providers to understand how these factors can increase someone's vulnerability to radicalisation.

In most cases, it is considered good practice to speak with the child or young person and their parents or carers. It is also good practice for DSLs to consider contextual, vulnerability and protective factors to make a comprehensive assessment of risk and harm. All concerns, discussions and decisions made, and the reasons for those decisions, should be recorded in writing.

Designated safeguarding leads are reminded to seek advice and support from Children's Social Care, the local authority Prevent partners or the police where necessary. With at risk concerns or cases which do not meet the threshold for a child in need or child protection statutory support or are not appropriate for Channel, it is best practice for providers to consider what protective interventions they might put in place to support the child or young person, as well as considering ongoing measures to build universal resilience to radicalisation within their settings.

'Interventions' are projects intended to divert people who are being drawn into terrorist activity. Interventions can include mentoring, counselling, theological support, encouraging civic engagement, developing support networks (family and peer structures) or providing mainstream services (education, employment, health, finance or housing).

For cases appropriate for Channel, the local authority multi-agency safeguarding approach will work in partnership to provide early help, statutory services, or intervention providers to support the child or young person.

Identifying radicalisation

'Radicalisation' is defined as the process by which a person comes to support terrorism and extremist ideologies associated with terrorists(CONTEST, 2018).

2. Risks of radicalisation

To safeguard children and young people vulnerable to radicalisation, designated safeguarding leads (DSLs) will need to undertake a risk-based approach. They should demonstrate an awareness and understanding of the risk of radicalisation in their area, institution or body. This risk will vary greatly and can change rapidly; but no area, institution or body is risk free (Revised Prevent Duty Guidance, 2019).^Contact your local policing team, local authority or safeguarding children partnership to understand the risks or threats in your area. You may also have a Prevent Coordinator or Prevent Education Officer in your local authority or a HEFE Regional Prevent Coordinator who can advise you.^

2.1 The threat of terrorism

'Terrorism' is defined as an action or threat designed to influence the government or intimidate the public. Its purpose is to advance a political, religious or ideological cause.

In summary this defines terrorism as:

  • an action that endangers or causes serious violence to a person/people
  • an action that causes serious damage to property, or seriously interferes or disrupts an electronic system
  • designed to influence the government or to intimidate the public and is made for the purpose of advancing a political, religious or ideological cause.

The Prevent Duty provides a framework for specified authorities to respond to the changing nature of threat in the UK. According to the government's current Counter Terrorism strategy, the main threat to the UK comes from Daesh (ISIS) or Al Qa'ida inspired terrorism, although Extreme Right-Wing terrorism is a growing threat (CONTEST, 2018).

Some groups and organisations are proscribed, which means that they are banned under counter-terrorism measures introduced under the Terrorism Act 2000 (for example, Da'esh and National Action).

The Home Office have published a list of proscribed terrorist groups or organisations

2.2 The extremism threat

The CONTEST strategy defines 'Extremism' as vocal or active opposition to the fundamental British values, including democracy, the rule of law, individual liberty and mutual respect andtolerance of different faiths and beliefs. Extremism also includes calls for death of members of the armed forces, whether in this country or overseas

There are other groups and organisations that promote extremist ideologies but are not proscribed terrorist groups or organisations. Such groups may espouse views which support distrust or hatred of those with different faiths or undermine principles of democracy. They may not promote extreme violence but support divisive or hateful narratives towards others.

2.3 Mixed, unclear, or unstable cases

Some children and young people may also appear engaged with, or have adopted, a mixed, unclear or unstable ideology, which supports extreme violence. For example, those who appear fixated with mass violence, such as school shootings or incels, whose intolerance is predominantly directed at women. This could include individuals who:

  • demonstrate an interest in multiple extremist ideologies in parallel
  • switch from one ideology to another over time
  • target a 'perceived other' of some kind (perhaps based upon gender or another protected characteristic), but do not otherwise identify with one particular terrorist ideology or cause
  • are obsessed with massacre, or extreme or mass violence, without specifically targeting a particular group (e.g. 'high school shootings')
  • may be vulnerable to being drawn into terrorism out of a sense of duty, or a desire for belonging, rather than out of any strongly held beliefs

3. How individuals become vulnerable to radicalisation

3.1 Vulnerability factors

Vulnerability factors could increase an individual's vulnerability to radicalisation. DSLs should understand this can be a complex process and that these are only potential indicators.

There is no single way of identifying whether a child or young person is likely to be susceptible to an extremist ideology or vulnerable to radicalisation. The process of radicalisation is different for every individual and can take place over an extended period or within a very short time frame. Adverse childhood experiences, combined with specific influences from family and peers or online connections, may contribute to a child or young person's vulnerability.

DSLs need to understand what radicalisation means and why children and young people may be vulnerable to being drawn into terrorism according to the Prevent Duty Guidance.

Factors that could make someone vulnerable to radicalisation include:

  • being a victim or witness of crime, abuse or bullying
  • being a perpetrator of crime, abuse or bullying
  • lacking a safe or stable home environment
  • experiencing social isolation or difficulties
  • having personal or emotional difficulties
  • holding previous criminal convictions and / or being prone to any erratic, violent, self-destructive or risky behaviours, or alcohol / drug misuse or dependency
  • being a looked after child or having previously been a looked after child
  • having special educational needs, developmental or behavioural needs, or mental ill health
  • expressing feelings of injustice or grievance involving any racial, religious or political issue, or conspiracy theories

Extremist influences can exploit or manipulate the personal or emotional vulnerabilities of children and young people. Children and young people who are vulnerable to grooming for child sexual exploitation, criminal exploitation or county lines, may also be vulnerable to radicalisation.

Extremist influences could include but are not limited to:

  • family members having direct contact or involvement with extremist or terrorist groups
  • staff members of an educational or community setting promoting an extremist ideology
  • peers promoting an extremist ideology or sharing extremist material
  • access or exposure to online extremist material via social media or the internet- i.e. propaganda including pictures, videos, blogs, and fake news stories etc.
  • exposure to extremist, terrorist or other violent activity in overseas settings
  • access or exposure to extremist leaflets, magazines or stickering
  • exposure to extremist groups hosting marches, protests or stalls

4. Online radicalisation

Children and young people are at risk of accessing inappropriate and harmful online extremist content. This could include downloading or sharing terrorist material, which could be a criminal act. The internet and social media enable people to reach many individuals and easily spread divisive and hateful narratives. Social media (apps, forums, blogs, chat rooms etc.) is used by extremist and terrorist groups or organisations to identify and target vulnerable individuals. DSLs do not need to be online experts to understand when someone is at risk of harm and should consider and address harmful behaviour online in the same way as offline activity.

4.1 Concerns that a child or young person is being radicalised online

Any child who uses the internet can be at risk of online abuse. It is important education settings are aware of the risks and talk to children and young people about staying safe online. If you are concerned that a child or young person is vulnerable to radicalisation online, you should follow your normal safeguarding procedures. Radicalisation is similar to grooming and whether this happens online or offline the concern should be seen and treated in the same way. You do not need to be an expert in social media or the online space to recognise and respond to concerning behaviour.

5. Extra-familial risk

The risk of extremism and radicalisation can be present within the family, but DSLs should be aware of other extremist influences such as online, peers, and wider environmental and community factors. Providers will need to consider a contextual safeguarding approach as explained in the Keeping Children Safe in Education guidance.

6. Risk factors

Someone may not be at risk of extremism or radicalisation solely due to the factors listed below. Often there are several risk factors present that, seen together, can cause concern.

6.1 Push factors

Push factors might be due to vulnerabilities. Push factors may include a child or young person feeling:

  • a sense of isolation
  • a lack of belonging
  • a lack of purpose
  • low self-esteem
  • their aspirations are unmet
  • a sense of anger or frustration
  • a sense of injustice
  • confused about life or the world
  • real or perceived personal grievances

Push factors related to ideology could include an extremist or terrorist group, organisation or individual:

  • ffering a sense of community and a support network
  • promising fulfilment or excitement
  • emphasising sense of the child or young person being special, grand and part of a wider mission
  • ffering a very narrow, manipulated version of an identity often subscribing to stereotypical gender-norms

6.2 Pull factors

Pull factors could include an extremist or terrorist group, organisation or individual:

  • ffering a sense of community and a support network
  • promising fulfilment or excitement
  • emphasising sense of the child or young person being special, grand and part of a wider mission
  • ffering a very narrow, manipulated version of an identity often subscribing to stereotypical gender-norms

Pull factors related to ideology could include:

  • ffering inaccurate answers or falsehoods to grievances
  • encouraging conspiracy theories
  • promoting an 'us vs. them' mentality
  • blaming specific communities for grievances
  • encouraging using hatred and violent actions to seek justice
  • encouraging ideas of supremacy

7. Radicalisation risk indicators

There is no single route to radicalisation. However, there are some behavioural traits that could indicate that a child or young person is vulnerable to radicalisation.

DSLs are reminded that radicalisation is not a linear process. Individuals may express a combination of behaviours at different times, and DSLs should consider an individual's behaviour in the context of wider influencing factors and vulnerabilities.

The following guidance is designed to help providers consider indicators of risk and decide what response is appropriate. These indicators are a guide to assisting the exercise of professional judgement. If in doubt, providers should make a referral.

Young people can move very quickly between the risk categories. Any escalation of risk should be dealt with immediately through the processes outlined below and recorded in the young person's file.

7.1 Low risk

At this stage there is no evidence to suggest that the child or young person is vulnerable to radicalisation. Behaviours which seen alone would not necessarily require further exploration or challenge. A child or young person may:

  • hold strong opinions or values (non-violent/non-extremist)
  • criticise government policies
  • adopt visible signs e.g. wearing clothing (non-violent/non-extremist) to express identity or sense of belonging
  • active on social media
  • take a keen interest in national and international affairs
  • take a stand and supports causes e.g. animal rights (non-violent)
  • show new interest in a political ideology or religion
  • hold or express conservative values or practices whether traditional, cultural or religious (unless they cause cause harm to a child or others e.g. FGM)

7.2 At risk

At this stage there is no evidence to suggest that the child is vulnerable to radicalisation, however there are concerns that a child or young person may be at potential risk in the future due to the presence of identified vulnerability factors or warning signs.

At risk behaviour of a child or young person that should be explored or challenged by the education setting and may warrant a Prevent referral. A child or young person may be:

  • drawn to conspiracy theories
  • begins to isolate themselves from family and friends
  • has been viewing or engaging with inappropriate online content and has uncontrolled or unsupervised access to the internet
  • expresses concerns about being victimised e.g. feeling under attack
  • discriminatory about other individuals or groups of people
  • sudden change in behaviour
  • shows interest in extremists or extreme groups
  • hides a new lifestyle or belief system from friends and family
  • expresses views that divide us e.g. speaks of 'us' and 'them'

7.3 Medium risk

There is evidence to suggest that a child or young person may be targeted and exploited by extremists.

Concerning behaviour that would warrant a Prevent referral includes:

  • legitimises the use of violence to defend ideology or cause
  • accesses extremist or terrorist website, forums and publications
  • expresses dehumanising views
  • expresses an interest to travel to a conflict zone
  • is in contact with a group or individuals known to support a violent extremist ideology either online or in real life
  • targets a 'perceived other' of some kind (perhaps based upon gender or another protected characteristic), but do not otherwise identify with one particular terrorist ideology or cause
  • is obsessed with massacre, or extreme or mass violence, without specifically targeting a particular group (e.g. 'high school shootings')

7.4 High risk

There is evidence that a child or young person is currently exposed to terrorist or extremist activity and the risk to the child or young person's safety is significant.

Activity that may be considered criminal and the police should immediately be informed includes:

  • verbally or physically attacks someone due to their race, religion, sexuality etc.
  • commits violent acts guided by a violent extremist ideology or group
  • takes part in any proscribed violent extremist group (financing, sharing material online, recruiting others etc.)
  • has a 'kill list' or detailed plan to conduct mass violence
  • produces or shares terrorist material offline or online
  • recruits others to a proscribed terrorist group or organisation

Staff respsonsibilities under Prevent

Make sure staff are aware of their responsibilities and know how to identify radicalisation.

7.5 The Prevent Duty

The Prevent Duty requires providers 'to have due regard to the need to prevent people from being drawn into terrorism'. This includes safeguarding children and young people from extremist ideologies and radicalisation. Having 'due regard' means that the authorities should place an appropriate amount of weight on the need to prevent people being drawn into terrorism when they consider all the other factors relevant to how they carry out their usual functions.

7.6 Staff responsibility in relation to the Prevent Duty

All staff are responsible for recognising any concerning changes in behaviour and reporting them to the DSL.

7.7 Behaviours that might indicate a child or young person is vulnerable to extremism or radicalisation

Any concerning changes in behaviour whether emotional, verbal or physical may indicate a safeguarding concern. Children and young people may be vulnerable in particular ways that could make them more susceptible to a range of harms including sexual exploitation, extremism and radicalisation or gang involvement. For more information refer to:

  • What makes someone vulnerable or susceptible to extremism or radicalisation?

  • Section 4: What are the potential indicators of extremism or radicalisation?

7.8 What should staff do if they are concerned that a child or young person is becoming involved in extremism or radicalisation

If staff have any concerns about a child's welfare, they should speak to the DSL immediately. Staff should refer to the setting's safeguarding procedures.

The Keeping Children Safe in Education guidance states that:

\>"All staff should know what to do if a child tells them he/she is being abused or neglected. Staff should know how to manage the requirement to maintain an appropriate level of confidentiality. This means only involving those who need to be involved, such as the designated safeguarding lead (or a deputy) and children's social care."

7.9 Where can staff access more information on Prevent and safeguarding children and young people from extremist ideology and radicalisation

Staff should be aware of:

  • Prevent Awareness eLearning from the Home Office
  • the Department for Education's Educate Against Hate website
  • any internal guidance or resources, such as a staff handbook

-

Staff in further education (FE) should be made aware of:

  • The Department for Education and the Education and Training Foundation's dedicated Prevent website for FE
  • staff awareness raising modules for FE and training.
  • staff should approach the DSL for further information.

8. Reporting extremising in education

The Local Authority Designated Officer (LADO) responds to allegations made about people who work with children. If you need to contact your LADO, you should contact your local safeguarding partnership or local authority. Where extremism concerns linked to an educational professional are raised with the LADO, the DfE's Counter-Extremism Division should also be notified.

Online training for Prevent awareness

In addition to this guidance, professionals with safeguarding responsibilities may wish to complete the following online training packages:

  • Prevent Awareness eLearning: this offers an introduction to the Prevent Duty and explains how it aims to safeguard people vulnerable to radicalisation and to supporting terrorism or becoming terrorists themselves.
  • Prevent Referrals eLearning: this training is designed to make sure that when we share a concern that a vulnerable individual may be being radicalised, that referral is robust, informed and with good intention, and that the response to that concern is considered, and proportionate.
  • Channel Awareness eLearning: Depending on your needs this product will help you understand the objectives of the Channel programme, the working process, roles and responsibilities. It will share key learnings and best practice from those already involved around the country, and will allow you to work through example case studies so you can see the process in action.

Th e modules set out the requirements of the Duty in the FE and training sector and include case studies, which bring the Prevent Duty to life.

For further information, contact your local authority.
