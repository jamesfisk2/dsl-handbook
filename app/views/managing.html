Managing the risk of radicalisation in your educational setting

Risk assessment framework

If you are unsure about the level of need, you should be familiar with how to access advice from a wide range of professionals in Children's Social Care (contact details for local Children's Social Care can be accessed when you report child abuse to a local council). There may also be specific advice you can access through a local authority Prevent team or other Prevent partners. If in doubt, it is important to share concerns and make a referral.

1. Low risk of radicalisation

Questions for practitioners include:

  • does this individual need any other services or support?
  • does the setting provide opportunities to discuss controversial issues and build resilience to radicalisation?

1.1 Procedure

Staff should consider informally discussing these changes in behaviour with the child or young person.

Staff may want to discuss some of these themes as a class. For example, hosting a debate on a government policy or understanding online safety. There are resources to support schools and FE providers..

As stated in the Prevent Duty guidance, schools and further education providers should be safe spaces in which children and young people can understand and discuss sensitive topics, including terrorism and the extremist ideas that are part of terrorist ideology, and to learn how to challenge these ideas.

With regular Prevent training, staff should understand what radicalisation means and why people may be vulnerable to being drawn into terrorism.

2. At risk of radicalisation

Questions for practitioners to ask themselves include:

  • do I have enough information to make a comprehensive assessment?
  • what has happened in the past to trigger the incident?
  • is this an isolated incident or is it a pattern of behaviour?
  • what else do we know? Are there any vulnerability factors that are relevant?
  • are there any contextual factors that are relevant? For example, previous safeguarding concerns, behaviour/attendance/attainment, general wellbeing.
  • are there any protective factors? For example, supportive personal relationships with peers/family, environmental factors such as school/college/provider or home life.

2.1 Prodedure

Designated Safeguarding Leads (DSLs) will need to analyse the information and, where there are gaps or unanswered questions, gather additional information to make a comprehensive assessment of risk and harm.

Find a safe space to have a conversation with the individual who has presented some concerning behaviour. How should I speak to a child or young person at risk of radicalisation?

The setting or provider should discuss any concerns with parents or carers. What should the DSL say to parents or carers?

Safeguarding practitioners should make a holistic assessment of vulnerability, examining risk and protective factors as set out on page 24 of the statutory safeguarding guidance .

DSLs can always seek advice from Prevent partners who will be best placed to support you in making a referral, if appropriate. DSLs are not required to provide any identifying information of the individual. This is also the case if discussing concerns with a police officer. If in doubt, make a referral. Allconcerns, discussions and decisions made, and the reasons for those decisions, should be recorded in writing).

The child or young person requires intervention by any professional, parent or carer who has a good relationship with them. As in other areas of safeguarding, without any protective factors or protective interventions, children and young people may face poor life outcomes. The setting could carry out interventions to support the individual. What can settings or providers do to prevent and address the risk of radicalisation?

If risk is escalating, follow your Prevent referral procedures and read the guidance for medium or high-risk cases. How do I make a Prevent referral?

3. Medium risk of radicalisation

Questions for practitioners to ask themselves include:

  • is there reasonable cause to suspect that the child or young person is suffering or likely to suffer significant harm?
  • what are the risks? If these needs are not met, what may happen? What will the impact of this be on the child/young person? What are we worried about?
  • what else do we know? Are there any vulnerability factors that are relevant?
  • are there any contextual factors that are relevant? For example, previous safeguarding concerns, behaviour/attendance/attainment record, general wellbeing.
  • are there any protective factors? For example, supportive personal relationships with peers/family, environmental factors such as school/college/provider or home life.

3.1 Prodedure

Safeguarding practitioners should make an assessment that considers vulnerability, and examines risk and protective factors as set out in the statutory guidance.

Where a child is suffering, or is likely to suffer from harm, it is important that a referral is made immediately. This is a statutory requirement. How do I make a Prevent referral?

Each local authority should have its own referral process for providers to raise concerns about individuals vulnerable to radicalisation, which may mirror existing safeguarding referral processes. These partners may provide advice or forward the referral onto Channel as appropriate.

Channel is a multi-agency partnership which focuses on early intervention to provide support at an early stage to individuals identified as being vulnerable to being drawn into terrorism. For further information see What does Channel support look like?

DSLs should normally carry out an assessment to identify whether there are needs that should be met by more than one agency. For example,. CAMHS. If you are unsure, the local authority may suggest this on receipt of the Prevent referral.

All practitioners should aim to gain parental consent (if under 18) to share information but should be mindful of situations where to do so would place a child or young person at increased risk of harm. What should the DSL say to parents or carers?

4. High risk of radicalisation

Questions for practitioner to ask themselves include:

  • does the individual need support from more than one agency?
  • is the individual about to put themselves or others in danger?
  • is the individual at risk due to actions of their parents or carers or wider family members?
  • has the individual made violent threats to your setting?

4.1 Procedure

If it is suspected that a person is about to put themselves or others in danger by travelling to join a proscribed organisation, or appears to be involved in planning to carry out a criminal offence, this supersedes all local referral processes and the police should be immediately informed.

If it is suspected that a person is likely to commit an attack on your setting, please seek immediate support from the police and local authority. School and college security guidance is available to ensure your setting is safe.

5. How to speak to a child or young person vulnerable to radicalisation

If concerns have been shared about an individual's behaviour, then in most cases it would be appropriate to speak with the child or young person as soon as possible.

DSLs should ensure their approach is:

  • learner centred: Record what the individual has said in their own words and record any actions using clear descriptions. If speaking to the individual on a one-to-one basis, provide reassurance that you wish to clarify something they said or did;
  • responsive and inclusive: Ask open and specific questions anddo not embellish details when reporting but ask for clarity where necessary e.g. 'Can you explain what you mean by that as I did not understand?'. Do not make assumptions as to the nature of the individual's behaviour based on any aspects of their background or identity
  • Proactive: Do not promise confidentiality. Be clear with the child or young person about your next steps or actions. If you see, hear or are worried about anything, consider making a Prevent referral. How do I make a Prevent referral?

The following steps may help DSLs to discuss any concerning behaviour displayed by a child or young person. This should inform your assessment of risk and harm.

  • Build a rapport â€“ build on your knowledge of the child or young person by asking about some neutral topics.
  • Get them talking â€“ use TED:
    • T â€“ tell
    • E â€“ explain
    • D â€“ describe.
    • for example, "Tell me what happened in Maths today,"
  • Avoid direct confrontation of opinions or attitudes â€“ Don't say: 'you're wrong'. Young people could become defensive, agitated or withdrawn by this. Consider asking 'what made you feel like this?'.
  • Redirect them â€“ If you are finding it hard to discuss the child or young person's behaviour because they feel angry or emotional, try to redirect them. Consider asking 'tell me something funny that happened last week'.'
  • Start by raising concerns about their behaviour, not beliefs. Don't say: 'you've been expressing some concerning beliefs/ideas'. Do say: ''I am concerned because I've noticed you have become (angry/disengaged), what's going on?'
  • Make observational comments about their emotional/behavioural state, for example: You look exhausted/agitated/angry, what are you doing to relax?', this may lead to: 'Where do you go, who do you see?'.
  • Do not ask 'leading questions',such as: 'did you hear this at home?'- Instead, start your questions with who, what, when, where, how e.g. 'How do you feel aboutâ€¦?', 'what do you understand byâ€¦?', 'where did you hearâ€¦?', 'what would you do ifâ€¦?'.
  • Ask questions, get them to explain â€“ 'What made you think about these things?' or 'Can you give an example of that?', 'Can you explain that further?', 'Is there another point of view on that issue?'. The individual might not be able to see other points of view and have a fixed view of the world.
  • Get them to think about what they are saying â€“ 'I still don't understand, explain it differently for me'. 'What is fact and opinion in this statement?', 'How do you know thatâ€¦.? On what do you base your judgement?'.
  • Ask for clarification â€“ If you are not sure what the child or young person has said, ask for clarification and check their understanding of certain words â€“ 'What does X mean?'

Handling difficult or controversial discussions can be challenging. If this is a low-risk concern you might say, 'I don't know much about this, shall we research it together?' or 'shall we find someone who does know more about this? I would like to learn more'. If this is a medium-risk concern, you might say 'I need to speak to someone to see if we can find someone who knows a lot about this. They could talk to you and help you understand more about X'.

The Prevent duty states that having a safe space to discuss controversial topics is crucial to providing an opportunity for children and young people to share their views and understand the views of others. Speaking about these issues can help build resilience in children and young people to challenge extremist narratives. It may also prompt disclosures, which staff may otherwise not be aware of. The Educate Against Hate and Let's Talk About It websites offer resources to support you with this.

5.1 Communication difficulties

Special provision should be put in place to support dialogue with children or young people who:

  • have communication difficulties
  • are too young
  • are unable to communicate
  • cannot or will not explain
  • if the situation is unclear.

DSLs should refer to a child or young person's behaviour plan and the information available from any assessments, which may include visual cues to help facilitate discussion, such as picture exchange communication cards. The DSL should seek support from the SENCO. Further information on communicating with people with learning difficulties is available from Mencap.

5.2 Mixed ideologies

If the child or young person supports the use of violence but is not particularly interested in an extremist ideology or is interested in lots of ideologies, you should follow your usual safeguarding arrangements and, in this case, seek support or advice from your local authority and/or Prevent team. You can make a referral to Prevent for concerns such as these â€“ for example, mixed ideology where the individual shows an interest in multiple ideologies, such as white supremacy and involuntary celibate (incels), or it could be unclear or unstable; this includes school massacre concerns (the desire to kill members of staff or learners).

5.3 Criminal offences

If it is suspected that a person is about to put themselves or others in immediate danger or appears to be involved in planning to carry out a criminal offence, this supersedes all local referral processes, and the police should be immediately informed.

6. What steps can I take to manage radicalisation and extremism safeguarding concerns?

  1. Find out your Prevent referral process in your local authority â€“ this should be on your local authority website (search for Prevent or Channel referral process).

  2. Establish effective partnerships with:

  • local policing team (you can find this by searching your area and entering your postcode on the relevant police forces website)
  • local authority or safeguarding children partnership
  • local authority Prevent lead
  • you may also have a local authority Prevent Education Officer
  • if you are an FE institution you should contact your HEFE Regional Prevent Coordinator

  1. Ensure you have the contact details for partners:Local policing team:Local authority Children's or Adult's Services or safeguarding children partnership:Local authority Prevent lead: Local authority Prevent Education Officer (if applicable):HEFE Regional Prevent Coordinator (if applicable):3. Contact partners to understand the local risk and threats in your area.

  2. Ensure you understand what radicalisation means and why children and young people may be vulnerable to being drawn into terrorism. Go to: How do people become radicalised?

  3. Ensure you know how to access advice from a wide range of professionals in Children's Social Care and /or seek support from local Prevent teams or police when needed.

  4. Ensure you are familiar with the support available for someone at risk of radicalisation. Go to: Multi-Agency Working

  5. Consider what support your institution can provide for someone vulnerable to radicalisation.8. Consider how your institution provides opportunties in the curriculum to build resilience to radicalisation for all learners.
